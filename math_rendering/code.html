Explain the time-independent Schrödinger equation and show its application in a basic 1D infinite potential well. Include the energy levels.

auto_awesome
Framr Thinking

The time-independent Schrödinger equation is the cornerstone of non-relativistic quantum mechanics. It describes the stationary states of a quantum system where the probability density remains constant over time.

\[ \hat{H}\psi = E\psi \]

In one dimension, where \( \hat{H} \) is the Hamiltonian operator, the equation expands to:

\[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2\psi(x)}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi(x) = E\psi(x) \]

The 1D Infinite Potential Well

Consider a particle of mass \( m \) trapped in a well where the potential \( V(x) = 0 \) for \( 0 < x < L \) and \( V(x) = \infty \) elsewhere. The boundary conditions require that the wavefunction \( \psi(x) \) must vanish at the walls: \( \psi(0) = \psi(L) = 0 \).

Solving the differential equation leads to normalized wavefunctions of the form:

\[ \psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \dots \]

The corresponding energy levels are quantized, depending on the square of the integer quantum number \( n \):

\[ E_n = \frac{n^2 \pi^2 \hbar^2}{2mL^2} \]

lightbulb Key Insight

Notice that the energy \( E_n \) can never be zero, even at the lowest state \( n=1 \). This is known as the zero-point energy, a direct consequence of the Heisenberg uncertainty principle.
